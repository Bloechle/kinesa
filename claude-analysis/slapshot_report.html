Hockey slap shot — three-subject comparison

JulienMatteoRemo · 6 takes per subject  ·  Motive @ 360 Hz  ·  Stick rigid body for impact  ·  All R-shooters (bottom hand = right)
Three slap shots, three signatures. Remo (self-labelled expert) reaches a typical stick speed of 27.4 m/s, clearly above Matteo (self-labelled middle, 22.1 m/s) and Julien (20.7 m/s). All three are right-shooters — bottom hand is the right hand on the stick. The X-factor and kinematic-chain signatures show what produces the speed gap: Remo loads the trunk further, releases later, and times the chain more cleanly. The most striking finding, though, is on backswing duration (Fig. 5): Remo loads for ~750 ms — textbook slap-shot wind-up — while Matteo and Julien both load for ~350 ms, half as long. Two of the three subjects appear to be executing a shorter shot motion than the slap-shot the protocol intended.

Synthesis. Three findings. (i) Output: Remo is clearly the fastest shooter (27.4 m/s typical), Julien and Matteo are very close to each other (20.7 vs 22.1 m/s) despite Matteo's self-label as 'middle' implying a clear ordering. The self-labels are useful framing but the data show two performance tiers, not three. (ii) Mechanism: Remo's advantage is body-driven, not arm-driven. The kinematic-chain cascade (Figs. 2 & 3) is staggered and clean for him, compressed for Matteo, and intermediate for Julien. The X-factor loading (Fig. 4) follows the same ordering: Remo ≈ 71°, Matteo ≈ 52°, Julien ≈ 44°. Same body, more body engaged in the shot. (iii) Backswing duration tells a different story than stick speed: Remo's wind-up is ≈ 780 ms (textbook slap-shot — stick comes high above the shoulder, takes time to load); Matteo's and Julien's are both ≈ 350 ms, half as long. That's wrist-shot or snap-shot territory, not slap-shot. The two less-experienced subjects appear to be executing a shorter, compressed shot motion regardless of intent — possibly the biomechanical signature of 'I tried to slap-shot but my body fell back to the shot type I'm comfortable with'. (iv) Repeatability: Remo's CV on stick peak is the lowest of the three (2.8% vs Matteo 5.7%, Julien 10.1%); his dots in Fig. 5 cluster tightest across all three metrics. Faster, body-driven, longer wind-up, and more repeatable — the four signatures of the more skilled shooter appear together rather than as separable axes.
Limits of this analysis. Limits of this analysis. n = 1 subject per skill level. The self-labels 'expert' / 'middle' come from the Motive skeleton names, not an independent assessment. Five to six takes per subject give a fair within-subject picture but between-subject gaps could partly reflect height, fitness on the day, or warm-up state. All three subjects are R-shooters (bottom hand = right) — detected from hand-Y position on the stick at impact, which is a more reliable signal than peak hand speed for two-handed grips (both hands on the same implement produce comparable speeds). Stick impact is detected from peak rigid-body speed (proxy for puck-contact; no puck was tracked); error ≈ 1–2 frames (3–6 ms).

For Matteo, three near-identical post-processed versions of take _004 (Matteo_004, Matteo_004-new, Matteo_004_newversion) shared the same captureStartTime and were therefore the same physical recording with different post-processing. Only Matteo_004.json was kept; the other two would have artificially inflated his consistency by counting one performance as three.

Data-quality probe: worst NaN coverage on a key bone inside the [-500, +300] ms ROI was 11.11% on remo_000 / Hip — above the 1% guideline threshold. However the longest consecutive NaN run is 1 frame: 32 isolated single-frame dropouts distributed across the window, which the Savitzky-Golay smoother handles by interpolation. The take's derived metrics (stick_peak 27.53 m/s, X-factor 74°, backswing 775 ms) sit cleanly within Remo's other 5 takes' ranges, so the dropouts do not appear to have corrupted the analysis. The two threshold definitions (% coverage vs longest run) tell different stories here: many isolated drops vs few long drops. The second is the one that actually breaks downstream metrics; the first is a useful warning. All takes had ≥100 ms slack before the ROI start.

Outlier check (±40% from subject median, n=5, with noise-floor escape): Matteo/Matteo_002 (backswing_ms=700.0, +91% from median 366.7). Remo/remo_003 (xf_loading=114.8, +59% from median 72.3). Each outlier is excluded from its specific metric aggregate only.
